---

Your app goes offline for 90 minutes. You reconnect.

A naive collaborative editor responds with a 97-second CPU freeze. Here is the exact mechanism, and how CRDTs fix it.

[1/6]

---

5 engineers editing a shared doc. One goes offline for 90 min.

Offline engineer: 1,800 edits.
Online team: 5,400 edits.

Operational Transformation (OT) reconnect math: 1,800 x 5,400 = 9.7 million transform pairs. At 10 microseconds each: 97 seconds of freeze on a laptop. 16 minutes on a 2021 iPhone.

Google Wave died partly from this.

[2/6]

---

Why OT gets stuck:

1. OT tracks position numbers. "Insert at position 5." But positions shift the moment anyone inserts to your left. Reconciling two diverged histories means transforming every pair of ops. Quadratic cost.

2. OT requires a central server to serialize all ops. No true offline editing. Server down, collaboration down.

[3/6]

---

CRDTs flip the model:

1. Every character gets a permanent ID and carries the IDs of its left and right neighbors at insertion time. Not a position number. Those IDs never shift, no matter what anyone inserts or deletes around them.

2. Two people inserting after the same character: a deterministic tiebreak by client ID resolves it on both clients locally. No server arbitration.

[4/6]

---

The reconnect, redone.

Offline engineer reconnects. Y.js sends a state vector: a tiny map of the last op ID seen from each collaborator. Server sends back exactly the 7,200 ops missed. Each applied in O(1): hash table lookup, linked list splice. Under 2 seconds.

OT: O(9.7 million). CRDT: O(7,200).

[5/6]

---

The trade-off to know:

Deleted characters leave tombstones in the linked list. After 10,000 insertions and 9,500 deletions, the CRDT holds all 10,000 nodes in memory: 9,500 invisible to the user. GC only when every replica confirms it saw the deletion.

Production stack: Y.js or Automerge. Cloudflare Durable Objects per doc as the sync server.
